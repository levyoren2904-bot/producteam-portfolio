## Context

Intelligence operations depend on having the right people, capabilities, and assets in the right place at the right time—often with incomplete or rapidly changing information.

Before Blue Wolf, planning and deploying forces for intelligence missions involved a mixture of spreadsheets, legacy tools, and informal communication channels. Commanders had to mentally reconcile unit readiness, location, skills, and mission requirements, often under tight time constraints.

Blue Wolf was designed to centralize this process into a single operational system—one place to understand who is available, what capabilities they bring, and how deployment decisions impact the wider operational picture.

## Challenge

The project required designing an interface that enables **precise decision-making under uncertainty**, balancing:

- **Risk** – What is the operational and intelligence risk of deploying or holding back a given unit?
- **Availability** – Which forces are actually ready, where are they, and what other commitments do they have?
- **Mission objectives** – How does each deployment decision contribute to or constrain current and planned missions?

The system needed to allow commanders to reason about multiple missions, timeframes, and contingencies without losing situational clarity.

## Constraints

Blue Wolf operated within several constraints:

- Information about missions, units, and capabilities is often sensitive or compartmentalized; not every user can see the full picture.
- Availability and readiness data comes from multiple systems that update at different cadences and levels of reliability.
- Deployment decisions frequently involve trade-offs—supporting one mission may degrade readiness for another.

These constraints meant that Blue Wolf had to be explicit about **what is known, what is assumed, and what is at risk**.

## Strategy

Our strategy for Blue Wolf focused on turning complex force-management decisions into a structured, explainable process.

<ProcessStep number="01" title="Mission-Resource Mapping">
We modeled missions and resources as first-class objects, making the relationship between objectives, required capabilities, and available units fully explicit in the interface.
</ProcessStep>

<ProcessStep number="02" title="Readiness as a Primary Signal">
Readiness and availability were elevated to primary signals in the UI, not secondary attributes—ensuring that every deployment decision is framed in terms of its impact on overall readiness.
</ProcessStep>

<ProcessStep number="03" title="Decision Support, Not Automation">
Blue Wolf surfaces trade-offs, conflicts, and potential gaps, but leaves the final judgment to commanders. The interface is designed to explain why a recommendation or warning exists, not to make opaque \"black-box\" suggestions.
</ProcessStep>

## Architecture

Blue Wolf’s interface is structured across four core views:

**Mission Planning Board**

A workspace for defining and adjusting intelligence missions, including objectives, timeframes, required capabilities, and priority. This board shows which missions are fully staffed, partially covered, or at risk.

**Force Readiness Overview**

An at-a-glance view of units, teams, and key capabilities—their readiness state, current location, and upcoming commitments. This view is optimized for understanding the global impact of deployment decisions.

**Assignment & Simulation Workspace**

Interactive tools for assigning units to missions, running \"what-if\" scenarios, and seeing how changes affect both individual missions and overall readiness. Conflicts and gaps are surfaced immediately.

**Operational Timeline**

A temporal view of missions, deployments, and recovery periods, helping planners ensure that today’s decisions do not compromise tomorrow’s operations.
